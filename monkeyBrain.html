What I'm trying to get at is that these images are simultaneously utterly irreplacable and utterly regular. So what if instead of going to these astronomical lengths to photograph this beautiful grid of pixels, or even the pale blue dot photo, what if we did it the other way around: just shook up a frame of pixels until it made something?

In theory*, there is nothing stopping us from randomizing 120,000 pixels and creating that exact pale blue dot photograph, pixel for pixel, without ever having to send the Voyager into space. We could also create a photorealistic image of you and Amelia Earhart shaking hands, the inside of your heart, the location you will be when you finish reading this. Or, of course, just random noise.

It's Not Easy Being Random

Scientists and experimental artists, being who they are, realized they had to one-up philosophy and actually try the Infinite Monkeys thought experiment, the final, published work of which you can find right here. As you're expect, the results are... less than shakespearean. That's because Monkeys aren't a good source of randomness. And this is to be expected, even humans aren't very good at being random. Thoughts don't come from a vacuum, but from our previous train of thought and our environment, which is why it's so hard to not think of a purple elephant when I ask you not to. Even computers, who are very often relied on to be random, are only really really good at faking randomness. Computers are, after all, deterministic machines by design, which is why random number generation is often more properly called Pseudo Random Number Generation (PRNG). If you take a hard to guess number, like the current time in milliseconds, and perform a really complicated, jumbling function on it, you can get a pretty random, hard to predict number. If you want to learn more about randomness (otherwise known as stochasticity), I highly reccomend this stupendous radiolab episode. But that's not the point at hand: even if we had a perfectly random machine, there is a stronger current we are pushing against.

When An Unstoppable Force Meets a Sheet of Tinfoil

One single pixel is composed of a tiny red, green, and blue light, and by varying the values of these base colors, a pixel blends the light together into what our eyes see as a single, distinct color. Each of the three base colors has a value from 0 to 255 (28) totalling 16,582,375 (2553) possible different colors. The dimensions of the 1 Monkey App are 32 x 42, totalling 1,344 pixels. What this means is that the number of possible blocky images that are possible in 1 Monkey's tiny frame are 16,582,3751,344. Here's what that number looks like. numberphile digits of pi the power of combinatorials One single pixel has a red, green, and blue channel light in it, each of which has 255 possible values. which means there are 16.5 million possible discreted colors. In this gamut is a lot of things. The color red, the color blue, the color of grass, the color of your eye (or the closest approximation).

Sure, the pale blue dot is just as likely as the 1 Monkey's starting screen, but that doesn't mean that all kinds of portraits are equally likely. Calculate likelihood of black and white photograph vs any other.
